# Understanding Cryptocurrencies

Bitcoin was created in 2008 by an anonymous person or group using the pseudonym Satoshi Nakamoto. The whitepaper titled "Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System" introduced a decentralized digital currency that operates without central banks or intermediaries.

## Blockchain Technology

A blockchain is a distributed ledger that records all transactions across a network of computers. Each block contains a cryptographic hash of the previous block, creating an immutable chain. Bitcoin's blockchain processes roughly 7 transactions per second, while Visa handles around 24,000.

The consensus mechanism Bitcoin uses is called Proof of Work, where miners compete to solve complex mathematical puzzles. The first miner to solve the puzzle adds the next block and receives newly minted bitcoin as a reward. This reward started at 50 BTC and halves approximately every four years.

## Ethereum and Smart Contracts

Ethereum was proposed by Vitalik Buterin in 2013 and launched in 2015. It introduced smart contracts—self-executing programs stored on the blockchain that automatically enforce agreements when conditions are met.

In September 2022, Ethereum transitioned from Proof of Work to Proof of Stake in an event called "The Merge." This reduced Ethereum's energy consumption by approximately 99.95% according to the Ethereum Foundation.

## Decentralized Finance

DeFi applications allow users to lend, borrow, and trade without traditional financial intermediaries. The total value locked in DeFi protocols peaked at over $180 billion in late 2021 before declining significantly.

Uniswap, launched in 2018, pioneered the automated market maker model where liquidity providers deposit token pairs into pools. The protocol has processed over $1.8 trillion in trading volume since its launch.

## Regulatory Landscape

The SEC has classified most cryptocurrencies as securities, with notable exceptions for Bitcoin and Ethereum. The landmark SEC v. Ripple case in 2023 established that programmatic sales of tokens on exchanges do not necessarily constitute securities transactions.
